Introduction back to ToC

OPINO (OPINion ONtology) is an ontology for the formal representation of opinions as Knowledge Graphs, grounded in the Description and Situation (DnS) Ontology Design Pattern (Gangemi and Mika 2003) and the DOLCE+DnS Ultralite foundational ontology (DUL). OPINO adopts DnS as its core architectural backbone. Its primary purpose is to serve as a domain-agnostic interface for representing opinions across heterogeneous contexts, without committing to a specific representational paradigm. In this context, an opinion is understood as a perspective-dependent description of an entity, premised on a construed state of affairs. Representations built with OPINO can be aligned to or converted into other models depending on the requirements of a given application. For instance, in the context of projects leveraging CRMinf as the primary model for representing propositional content, OPINO can work as a neutral interface layer that bridges the opinion's epistemic structure with CRMinf's inference and belief machinery. Similarly, OPINO representations can be mapped to Digital Hermeneutics or nanopublication-based architectures when layered provenance is required. The core class of OPINO is opino:Opinion, a subclass of dul:Description that represents a perspective-dependent description of an entity, premised on a construed state of affairs. The entity being described (via opino:describes) and the state of affairs that grounds the description (via opino:isPremisedOn) are distinct: the Opinion is a claim about an entity that results from the cognizer's construal of observable or documented facts. For instance, the opinion 'the Donation of Constantine is a forgery' describes the Donation of Constantine, and is premised on a Construal involving the presence of anachronistic Latin, feudal terminology, and inconsistent dating formulae — facts perceived and interpreted by the cognizer as evidence for the claim. A different cognizer may construe the same facts differently, or construe different facts entirely, producing a different Opinion about the same entity. An Opinion is not necessarily an interpretation in the hermeneutical sense. It may be as simple as 'Marco is good' or 'this painting is beautiful'. What distinguishes it from a neutral assertion of fact is its perspective-dependence: it is always the description of an entity as seen through the lens of a specific cognizer, grounded in what that cognizer perceives. The opino:Interpretation subclass is reserved for the specific case where an Opinion results from a deliberate hermeneutical interpretive act — formally represented as a hico:InterpretationAct in HiCO — involving a documented methodology, a set of criteria, and a source. An Interpretation inherits all the epistemic properties of Opinion while adding the full provenance layer provided by HiCO. Not every Opinion is an Interpretation: opinions formed in everyday discourse, automated extraction pipelines, or collective community processes may be generated by plain prov:Activity instances without the hermeneutical apparatus that HiCO models. An Opinion is satisfied by a Construal — a perspective-dependent dul:Situation that reifies the set of facts as perceived by the cognizer, following the notion of construal from cognitive linguistics and social psychology (Ross 1987; Langacker 2008). The Construal is valid only when interpreted from the standpoint of a specific agent: the same objective state of affairs may be construed differently by different cognizers, leading to different Opinions over the same entities. The Opinion is the description that emerges from the analysis of a Construal — it is what the cognizer concludes from what they perceive, directed at the entity the claim is about. Each Opinion carries an epistemic status, represented by an EpistemicStatusParameter valued by one of three EpistemicRegion individuals: AssertionValue (the holder commits to the claim as their current considered interpretation), HypothesisValue (the holder commits tentatively, pending further evidence), or ConjectureValue (the holder entertains the claim as a possibility without sufficient evidence to assert it). This determines whether an Opinion is classified as an opino:Assertion, opino:Hypothesis, or opino:Conjecture respectively, via owl:equivalentClass definitions. Each Opinion is also tied to the provenance of its production via prov:wasGeneratedBy, which relates it to a prov:Activity. When the generating activity is a hico:InterpretationAct — as defined by the Historical Context Ontology (HiCO; Daquino and Tomasi 2015) — the Opinion is equivalent to an opino:Interpretation. HiCO provides the full provenance context of the interpretive act: the agent who performed it, the time, the methodology, and the source document. OPINO does not replicate this layer but bridges to it via the provenance relation. Agreement, disagreement, and support between Opinions are deliberately kept outside the OPINO model. These dynamics are social and community-level epistemic phenomena, appropriately handled at the level of the prov:Activity node using CiTO properties (cito:agreesWith, cito:disagreesWith, cito:citesAsEvidence) as already provided by HiCO's integration with CiTO. OPINO focuses exclusively on the epistemic structure of the individual claim. The properties of an Opinion are: - opino:isPremisedOn — relates an Opinion to the Construal that constitutes its premises (subproperty of dul:isSatisfiedBy) - opino:hasEpistemicStatus — relates an Opinion to its EpistemicStatusParameter (Functional; subproperty of dul:hasParameter) - opino:heldBy — shortcut relating an Opinion directly to its holder (range: owl:Thing; subproperty of dul:associatedWith) - opino:describes — relates an Opinion to the entity its propositional content is about (subproperty of dul:describes) - opino:usesConcept — relates an Opinion to the concept being predicated of its subject (subproperty of dul:usesConcept) - prov:wasGeneratedBy — relates an Opinion to the prov:Activity that produced it Other properties of DUL can also be used to further describe the Opinion (such as dul:introduces). The core ones (opino:isPremisedOn, opino:describes, opino:usesConcept) were instantiated into OPINO for clarity, as subproperties of their DUL counterparts scoped to opino:Opinion as domain. The properties of a Construal are: - opino:grounds — relates a Construal to the Opinion it supports, as premises support a conclusion (inverse of isPremisedOn; subproperty of dul:satisfies) - opino:involves — relates a Construal to any entity — object, agent, event, or abstract concept — that the cognizer perceives as participating in the construed state of affairs (subproperty of dul:isSettingFor). When a Construal satisfies a Framester frame via dul:satisfies, the roles of the involved entities can be made explicit using Framester frame element properties (e.g. fe:Artifact.forgery, fe:Means.forgery), providing a more granular representation without requiring modification of the core pattern. - opino:perceivedBy — relates a Construal to the cognizer whose perspective constitutes it (range: owl:Thing; subproperty of dul:associatedWith) - opino:hasPart — relates a Construal to its constituent sub-Construals, each representing an individual premise (subproperty of dul:hasPart; allValuesFrom opino:Construal) - opino:presupposes — relates a Construal to another Construal that must hold as a logical or epistemic premise before it (subproperty of dul:hasPrecondition) - opino:enables — relates a Construal to another Construal it makes possible as a subsequent inferential step (subproperty of dul:hasPostcondition) - dul:satisfies — optionally relates a sub-Construal to a dul:Description, typically a Framester frame (Gangemi et al. 2016), providing the conceptual vocabulary through which the construed state of affairs is interpreted A Construal can be extended in two ways. First, by converting it into a TimeIndexedConstrual — a subclass of opino:Construal equivalent to a Construal that has at least one opino:holdsAtTime relation pointing to a dul:TimeInterval — for temporally bounded perceptions, such as 'yesterday Marco did something good' or 'this painting was attributed to Caravaggio during the period 1990–2005'. Second, by decomposing it into sub-Construals via opino:hasPart, each of which may itself be satisfied by a Framester frame describing the inferential role of that premise within the overall construal. Sub-Construals can be ordered via opino:presupposes and opino:enables to represent the logical sequence of the inferential chain. OPINO is informed by the CRMinf extension of CIDOC CRM, the Digital Hermeneutics model (Daquino, Pasqual and Tomasi 2021), and the SEBI ontology (https://valentinapasqual.github.io/sebi/).
